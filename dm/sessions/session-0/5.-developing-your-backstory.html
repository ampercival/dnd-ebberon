1. Developing Your Backstory

Now that we’ve explored Eberron’s rich history, diverse locations, and the unique people who populate its vast landscapes, it’s time to anchor your character within this living world. Each adventurer in Eberron has a story, shaped by the Last War, the shifting politics of the Five Nations, and the mystical forces that weave through their lives. This is your opportunity to craft a character deeply connected to this setting, whose goals, alliances, and past experiences are integral to their journey.

In this part, we will focus on how your character’s past is intertwined with the people and events around them. Whether shaped by the shared trauma of the Last War or driven by personal ambitions, your character’s backstory will provide the foundation for their future in Eberron. To help guide you through this process, we will cover the following topics:

  • Setting the Scene: Understand the current state of the world and where the campaign begins.
  • Your Group Patron: Korranberg Chronicle: Discover your shared patron and how it ties your party together.
  • Choosing Your Class: Select the class that defines your character’s abilities and role in the world.
  • Choosing Your Species: Choose a species that shapes your character’s culture, history, and worldview.
  • Choosing Your Alignment: Decide on an alignment that reflects your character’s moral compass while fitting with the group dynamics.
  • Exploring Faith and Beliefs: Determine the role of religion or philosophy in your character’s life and their connection to Eberron’s many belief systems.
  • Your Role in the Last War: Reflect on how the Last War affected your character and their journey after the conflict.
  • Creating Your Character’s Background: Build or select a background that summarizes your character’s experiences and the skills they’ve gained.
  • Establishing Character Goals: Set long-term, mid-term, and short-term goals that will drive your character’s actions and decisions.
  • Determining Connections with Other Characters: Explore potential relationships and shared histories with other party members to create a cohesive group.

As you develop your character, these sections will guide you in crafting a backstory that enriches your role in the world of Eberron, ensuring that your adventurer is fully integrated into its complex, magical, and politically charged environment.

Setting the Stage

The campaign begins two years after the end of the Last War. It is the month of Therendor, early spring, when the world seems to wake from its winter slumber. The trees along the streets and hillsides are budding with fresh green leaves, while wildflowers bloom in patches of vibrant color across the plains. The chill of winter lingers in the air, but each day grows warmer, with the scent of damp earth and new growth heavy on the breeze.

You find yourselves on Mol, the 2nd, the start of a typical week. The streets of the cities in Khorvaire hum with the usual activity, but the two-year anniversary of the Day of Mourning still lingers in your mind. It was only weeks ago, on 20 Olarune, that Khorvaire remembered the catastrophic event—the day Cyre was swallowed by a strange and terrible power. For most, it is a day to mourn the countless lives lost and the nation wiped from the map. But for survivors of Cyre, it is a scar that never fully heals. Every year, Cyrans gather to sing the old songs, to speak the names of the fallen, and to share stories of their once-great kingdom. Yet, not all of these gatherings are peaceful. Some survivors still burn with anger, a fury that has boiled over into skirmishes and acts of violence during this grim anniversary.

The Sun's Blessing is only a few weeks away, on the 15th of Therendor—a festival dedicated to Dol Arrah, the goddess of honor and sunlight. It is a time when all are urged to set aside their differences and seek peace. It is a day when the city of Sharn, in particular, basks in the warmth of Dol Arrah's grace. Visitors and residents alike find a rare sense of calm, and the usual tensions of city life ease for a brief moment. Many look forward to the festival, a brief respite in the shadow of the war’s long aftermath.

Your Group Patron: Korranberg Chronicle

DM Rule

Your characters will begin their adventures as part of the Korranberg Chronicle, a renowned newspaper in Eberron.

Group Patrons in Ebberon

In Eberron, patrons are powerful organizations, individuals, or institutions that guide and support adventurers in their quests. A patron gives your character a connection to the larger world, providing resources, direction, and a reason to take on missions. These patrons can range from dragonmarked houses and universities to noble rulers or secretive espionage agencies. Some patrons offer clear assignments and expect loyalty, while others act more as benefactors, allowing adventurers to pursue their own goals in exchange for occasional services. By working for a patron, your character gains purpose, opportunities, and connections that help shape their role in the world of Eberron.

About the Korranberg Chronicle

The Korranberg Chronicle is Khorvaire's most prominent and widely read newspaper, known for its balanced reporting and in-depth coverage of key events, including the Last War. It is published three times a week (Mol, Wir, and Far) and features stories ranging from breaking news across the continent to royal proclamations, business announcements, and tales of daring adventurers. The Chronicle's vast readership is supported by a distribution partnership with House Orien, which ensures swift delivery to major cities and towns across Khorvaire via lightning rail and mail services.

Offices and Staff: The main office of the Korranberg Chronicle is based in Korranberg, but it maintains field offices throughout the Five Nations, Mror Holds, and Zilargo. These offices share space with House Sivis message stations, allowing for rapid communication between reporters and headquarters. Reporters, freelancers, and correspondents span the continent, often operating in dangerous or remote areas. The paper's editorial team, largely made up of Zil gnomes, is divided into ranks based on seniority, with junior editors covering local beats and senior editors overseeing broader topics such as crime or international affairs.

Allies: The Chronicle has built relationships with powerful allies, including House Orien, which aids in its distribution network, and House Sivis, facilitating fast communication between offices and reporters. Additionally, the gnome nation of Zilargo, where the Chronicle is based, provides political support and influence, especially when needed to protect its employees.

Enemies: The Korranberg Chronicle's commitment to the truth has made it enemies, including the Boromar Clan, which holds a grudge after a past expose led to major arrests within the crime syndicate. In Karrnath, King Kaius blames the paper for setbacks during peace talks, leading to harassment of its reporters. Meanwhile, House Thuranni, whose private affairs have been uncovered one too many times, refuses to cooperate with the Chronicle under any circumstances. Despite these challenges, the Korranberg Chronicle remains a trusted source of news and a powerful force in the dissemination of information across Eberron.

Benefits

As members of the Korranberg Chronicle, your group enjoys several key benefits related to your role as reporters, adventurers, and investigators.

Compensation: Each member of your group earns 1 gp per day for providing the newspaper with publishable stories. This income is enough to sustain a modest lifestyle and covers your basic needs.

Expenses: In addition to your salary, the newspaper reimburses work-related expenses such as travel costs (for missions or interviews), meals during interviews, and communication fees (for couriers or House Sivis message stations). Excessive or frivolous expenses, however, might be refused.

Equipment: Your group can request access to the newspaper’s equipment, such as printing presses or communication tools. You may use this equipment for personal purposes, provided it doesn’t interfere with the newspaper’s operations or cross certain boundaries.

Press Access: Each member is issued identification papers that establish your role as a journalist. This status grants you some respect and can help you gain access to key figures, government officials, or other people of influence. However, revealing sensitive truths can put your group in danger from those who want to keep certain information hidden.

DM Rule

Add Press Identification Papers to your inventory.

Implications

Your role at the Korranberg Chronicle is more than just a job—it’s a vital part of your character’s identity and how they fit into Eberron’s complex world. As you craft your backstory, think about how your character’s experiences and personal goals are tied to their work at the Chronicle. Use the questions below to guide your decisions:

  • What is your role at the newspaper?
    Are you a junior reporter just starting to make a name for yourself, or are you more of a behind-the-scenes investigator or researcher? Perhaps you're still trying to prove yourself by taking on dangerous assignments.

  • Where is your office?
    Do you work out of the main office in Korranberg, where you are close to the editorial board, or have you been assigned to a smaller, more remote location, such as a field office in Breland or Thrane? Consider why you were placed there—was it your choice, or were you sent away for specific reasons?

  • How did you transition from the Last War to the Chronicle?
    The Last War only ended two years ago, and its effects are still felt across Khorvaire. Whether you were a soldier, spy, or civilian, think about how your character went from those chaotic times to working as a journalist. Did you take this job to leave the war behind, seeking a quieter life, or did something you experienced during the war—perhaps a crucial event or shocking revelation—drive you to uncover the truth? Your role at the Chronicle could be a way to make sense of your past, or it might simply be the next chapter in a journey shaped by the war’s aftermath.

  • What have you accomplished during your time at the Chronicle?
    Have you had any small victories or published pieces that were well received? Maybe you broke a minor story or built a useful network of contacts. Alternatively, are you still struggling to get your first big break?

  • What is your journalistic focus?
    If you’re a reporter or investigator, do you have a particular beat or area of expertise? Examples might include uncovering political corruption, writing human-interest stories, or investigating the activities of dragonmarked houses. Your journalistic focus could help shape the type of adventures your character embarks on and the risks they take to uncover the truth. Some ideas are in the tables below.

Additional Eberron Lore Species

However, Eberron is also home to species found only within its lore, offering you the opportunity to explore characters shaped by the unique forces and factions of this world.

You may also choose from the species featured in the official Eberron sourcebooks, these are:

  • Changeling
    Changelings are shapechangers who can alter their appearance at will, using this gift to create new personas or adopt masks for different situations, often living hidden among other races and following the unpredictable path of the Traveler.

  • Gargoyle
    Gargoyles are living stone beings originally crafted by Dhakaani goblins and animated by the daelkyr Orlassk, often taking on horned and winged forms, and driven by curiosity, guardianship, motion, or contemplation as they carve out a place in the world, particularly in Droaam.

  • Gnoll
    Gnolls are fierce and primal creatures, originally created as fiendish soldiers, but many have broken free of their demonic overlords, becoming loyal pack members and hunters, especially in Droaam, where some even harness their fiendish heritage as warlocks or demon-hunters.

  • Goblinoid (Goblin, Hobgoblin, Bugbear)
    Goblinoids, which include goblins, hobgoblins, and bugbears, once ruled Khorvaire under the mighty Dhakaani Empire, but today they are divided into distinct cultures such as the city-dwelling goblins, the Ghaal’dar tribes of Darguun, the raiding Marguul bugbears, and the secretive, martial Heirs of Dhakaan, each struggling to reclaim their former glory or carve out a place in a post-war world.

  • Half-Elf
    Half-elves, known as Khoravar, are the true children of Khorvaire, born from the union of humans and elves and raised in diverse communities with their own unique traditions, strengthened by the influence of the dragonmarked Houses Lyrandar and Medani.

  • Kalashtar
    Kalashtar are a unique people born from the union of humans and renegade quori spirits from the plane of dreams, granting them psionic abilities, a deep spiritual connection, and a constant struggle against the forces of the Dreaming Dark that seek their destruction.

  • Half-Orc
    Orcs and half-orcs in Eberron are descendants of ancient tribes who have long battled both the aberrations of the daelkyr and the fiends of the Demon Wastes, with many now playing key roles as Gatekeepers, mercenaries, or inquisitives working for House Tharashk, while others follow darker paths tied to the Dragon Below.

  • Harpy
    Harpies in Eberron are winged beings with the power to charm others through their magical voices, driven by intense passions and loyalty, with some serving the Daughters of Sora Kell in Droaam while others fiercely oppose them.

  • Medusa
    Medusas in Eberron are feared for their petrifying gaze and serve as powerful leaders in Droaam, where they are known for their skill in stonework and their devotion to the Dark Six, combining calm, calculating minds with a dangerous supernatural presence.

  • Shifter
    Shifters, sometimes called the "weretouched," are humanoids with a bestial heritage, able to temporarily enhance their animalistic traits through a transformation called shifting, embodying primal instincts and walking the line between civilization and the wilds.

  • Warforged
    Warforged, originally built as weapons for the Last War, are living constructs made of metal, wood, and stone, possessing sapience and emotion despite their mechanical nature, and now struggle to find purpose and identity in a world that no longer needs them for war.

  • Worg
    Worgs are intelligent, speech-capable predators with sharp senses and a deep bond to nature, often devoted to primal deities like the Devourer and the Fury, and while they lack hands, they can be outfitted with specialized armor and enchanted Steel Jaws to enhance their formidable bite.

Implications

When choosing your species, consider the broader impact it has on your character's identity and how it ties into the world of Eberron. Reflect on these questions and ideas to deepen your understanding of your character’s heritage and how it influences their outlook and abilities:

  • Where do they come from?
    Every species in Eberron has a unique history and place in the world. How does your character's origin influence their perspective? Are they from a close-knit community like changelings who hide in plain sight, or a war-torn land like the goblinoids of Darguun?
  • What cultures, traditions, and beliefs have they inherited?
    Each species carries rich traditions, from the spiritual devotion of the kalashtar to the primal instincts of shifters. How do these cultural legacies shape your character’s worldview? Do they embrace their heritage or struggle against it?
  • How has their lineage prepared them to face the challenges ahead?
    Your character’s species affects their abilities and approach to obstacles. Do they draw on the resilience of their warforged construction, the fierce loyalty of a gnoll, or the adaptability of a changeling? Consider how your character’s species gives them unique advantages or creates challenges they must overcome.
  • How does the rarity of their species affect them?
    Eberron’s population is dominated by humans, dwarves, elves, halflings, and gnomes. If your character is a member of a rarer species—such as a medusa, warforged, or gnoll—how does this shape their interactions with others? Do they face prejudice, curiosity, or misunderstanding? How have they navigated life as an outsider, and do they try to blend in or embrace their uniqueness?

Choosing Your Alignment

Alignment represents your character’s moral and ethical stance in the world of Eberron, providing a framework for how they make decisions, interact with others, and approach challenges. However, unlike in more traditional D&D settings, alignment in Eberron is not always black and white. The world is morally complex, and even the most righteous organizations, like the Church of the Silver Flame, have dark corners, while followers of sinister forces may act with kindness and honor.

DM Rule

Characters with an evil alignment are not allowed in this campaign.

Your character must be able to cooperate with the group and have motivations that fit the overall tone of the story. Consider creating a character who can work with others, even if they have different perspectives or goals.

Complexity in Eberron

In a setting shaped by intrigue, politics, and the aftermath of a devastating war, moral dilemmas are common. Characters may find themselves acting outside their alignment to achieve their goals or survive in a world where alliances shift and nothing is certain. Consider how your alignment might evolve as the story progresses.

Example Alignments in Eberron

  • Lawful Good: A knight devoted to the Silver Flame, upholding justice but struggling with the darker actions of the church.

  • Neutral Good: A former soldier who seeks peace and healing in a war-torn world, but will break the rules to help those in need.

  • Chaotic Good: A daring bard who fights for freedom and equality, using their talents to inspire rebellion against corrupt rulers and oppressive systems.

  • Lawful Neutral: A Karrnathi officer who values order above all else, enforcing the law without concern for morality.

  • True Neutral: A druid of the Eldeen Reaches, focused on maintaining the balance of nature, indifferent to the politics and wars of nations.

  • Chaotic Neutral: A rogue changeling who lives by their own rules, adopting new identities and following the whims of the moment.

Implications

Your alignment can influence how your character interacts with the world, as well as how NPCs or factions respond to them. While it doesn’t need to define every decision, it should guide your character’s general outlook. Consider the following questions as you choose your alignment:

  • What are your character’s personal morals?
    Do they value compassion, fairness, and honesty, or are they more concerned with self-preservation and ambition?
  • How do they view authority and rules?
    Does your character respect established laws and hierarchies, or do they prefer to challenge or ignore them in favor of personal freedom?
  • Will their alignment shift?
    Eberron’s morally complex world often pushes characters into difficult choices. Consider whether your character might undergo an alignment shift based on their experiences and the challenges they face.

Exploring Faith and Beliefs

In Eberron, faith and belief systems are woven into everyday life and can deeply influence your character’s background, motivations, and development. Whether your character is a devoted follower of an established religion like the Silver Flame or the Sovereign Host, a member of a mysterious cult, or someone questioning their faith, your character’s connection to divine power—or lack thereof—can shape their path. As you build your character, consider how their beliefs align with their experiences and how these beliefs might evolve through the challenges they face in the world of Eberron.

Organized Faiths

  • The Silver Flame: A tangible spiritual force that actively holds demons at bay. Followers of the Flame protect innocents from supernatural evil and encourage compassion. Think of it like a mix between the Jedi and the Men in Black — they draw power from the Flame to fight evil rather than worship an anthropomorphic god.
  • The Blood of Vol: This is a practical, yet grim, faith that rejects the idea of an afterlife, viewing the gods as cruel. Followers believe that all mortals possess a spark of divinity within their blood, and Seeker clerics and paladins draw power from their own souls rather than deities.
  • The Sovereign Host: A pantheon of deities whose presence is believed to guide their followers, though they don’t physically manifest in the world. This is the most popular religion in the Five Nations, and its casual faith asks little of its followers.
  • The Dark Six: Representing chaos, greed, and destruction, the Dark Six are feared across Khorvaire. They embody the darker aspects of existence, such as war, betrayal, and wild nature, and are typically shunned by society. However, cults and outcasts may secretly revere them, believing that power can be gained by embracing these darker forces.

Cults and Druid Sects

  • The Cults of the Dragon Below: These cults serve dark, often malevolent powers or follow bizarre, irrational beliefs. While outsiders lump them together under this name, members of these cults see themselves as serving a higher purpose, such as the "blessed eye of the All-Seer" or "pilgrims of the Inner Sun." Many cults unknowingly serve ancient entities like the daelkyr or fiendish overlords, while others follow forces beyond mortal comprehension. Though they often clash with each other, each cult believes its cause is righteous. There are many cults to choose from, and your DM can offer more details to guide you in choosing one.
  • Druid Sects: Druids in Eberron follow different sects, each with its own beliefs about nature, life, and the natural balance. These groups vary in purpose, from the Gatekeepers, who guard against extraplanar threats, to the Wardens of the Wood, protectors of the Eldeen Reaches. Druids can be found all across Eberron, from ancient forests to faraway planes, each sect offering a unique approach to the natural world. Your DM can provide more information on the sects that best fit your character.
Tip

The DM can provide you with more information about specific cults and sects.

Faithless

  • Faithless: Many people in Eberron are faithless, either rejecting the existence of gods or simply not caring whether they exist since the gods do not manifest physically. If your character is faithless, consider whether they lost their belief after a traumatic event or never had faith to begin with.

Implications

Your character’s faith, or lack thereof, can have significant implications for their interactions with others and how they view the world. In a setting like Eberron, where divine power is present yet the gods themselves remain distant, belief systems are a matter of choice and personal conviction rather than universal truth. Here are a few considerations:

  • Social Connections: If your character follows an organized faith like the Silver Flame or the Sovereign Host, they may have connections with others who share their beliefs, from local temples to powerful religious organizations. This could offer support or lead to friction if your character's actions are at odds with the teachings of their faith.

  • Conflict and Rivalry: Some religions, such as the Silver Flame and the Blood of Vol, have contentious relationships with each other. Your character’s beliefs might place them in direct opposition to others—whether it's political tension, ideological debate, or outright hostility. Characters aligned with darker cults or the faithless might face mistrust or even persecution in more devout communities.

  • Moral Dilemmas: A character’s faith can guide their moral compass, influencing decisions in times of crisis. Does a follower of the Silver Flame prioritize the greater good, even if it involves difficult choices? Does a Seeker of the Blood of Vol hold their personal survival above all else? Your faith may also evolve, especially if your character experiences events that challenge their beliefs.

  • Divine or Supernatural Power: If your character is a cleric, druid, or paladin, their faith may provide direct access to divine or natural magic. But even non-religious characters may interact with or oppose divine forces. Your connection to a higher power (or the rejection of one) can play a major role in the abilities you gain and the challenges you face.

  • Cultural Identity: In Eberron, faith is often tied to culture. For instance, followers of the Sovereign Host might align with traditional values, while members of the Blood of Vol may question established norms. How does your character’s faith fit into their cultural heritage, and how does it shape their interactions with others from different belief systems?

Your Role in the Last War

For most of the last century, the continent of Galifar was engulfed in a bitter civil war. If you’re human, you’ve never known a world without war. If you’re a warforged, you were literally built to fight in it. The Last War ended two years ago after the utter destruction of one of the warring nations.

Consider how the war shaped your character and their abilities. For instance, a rogue might have been a smuggler avoiding the war or an infiltrator working behind enemy lines.

Here are some key questions to help define your character’s wartime experience:

  • How were you involved in the war?
    • Were you a soldier, criminal, conscientious objector, or civilian?
    • Did your talents keep you off the front lines, or were you directly involved in battle?
    • In this magical world, wizards, artificers, and other classes all had roles on the battlefield. What was yours?
  • If you fought, how did your service end?
    • Are you still in good standing with the military, or did you leave that life behind?
    • Are you proud of your service, or would you rather forget it?
    • Was there a defining moment, like a heroic battle or a narrow escape from death?
  • How did the war affect you personally?
    • Did you lose a loved one or see your hometown destroyed?
    • If you’re from Cyre, how has the loss of your entire nation shaped you?
    • Did your wartime experiences challenge your faith, or did they strengthen it?
Tip

You don’t have to choose the "Soldier" Background just because you served in the war.

Establishing Character Goals

Establishing meaningful character goals is a key element in shaping a dynamic, engaging campaign. These goals serve as the driving force behind your character’s actions, giving purpose to their journey and setting the stage for the adventures to come. As you build your character’s backstory and personality, you’ll also want to craft goals that reflect their ambitions, fears, and desires. To make the most of your character’s goals, consider framing them across three time scales: long-term, mid-term, and short-term.

DM Rule

Create a long-term goal, a couple medium term-goals, and a few short-term goals.

Long-Term Goals

Long-term goals are the overarching objectives that define your character’s journey and often align with the broader story of the campaign. These goals are ambitious, sometimes life-changing, and could take the entirety of the campaign to achieve. Whether it's reclaiming a lost kingdom, seeking vengeance for a fallen comrade, or uncovering ancient secrets tied to the Prophecy, long-term goals give your character a central motivation. Think of these as the main arc of your character's story—something they’re striving towards as the ultimate expression of their desires or destiny.

  • Example: "I want to reclaim my family’s honor by proving our connection to the Silver Flame."

Mid-Term Goals

Mid-term goals bridge the gap between the grand ambitions of long-term goals and the more immediate needs of short-term tasks. These goals take time and effort to achieve, often involving multiple steps, but they help move the character closer to their ultimate objectives. A mid-term goal might require you to gain allies, gather resources, or confront powerful enemies along the way. They also provide key moments of character development, as your character grows and changes while working towards these milestones.

  • Example: "I need to find the sacred relic of my ancestors in order to unlock the true power of my family’s lineage."

Short-Term Goals

Short-term goals are immediate, actionable objectives that can typically be accomplished in a session or two. These are practical tasks your character might focus on as stepping stones toward their larger goals. Short-term goals might involve acquiring a specific item, solving a local problem, or getting closer to an important NPC. These goals keep the character active and involved in the day-to-day progression of the story while steadily advancing their broader aspirations.

  • Example: "I need to track down the local historian who might have a clue about the relic’s location."

Setting Achievable and Engaging Goals

When creating goals, it’s important to ensure they are both achievable and fun to pursue. A good goal is measurable—your character should know when they’ve succeeded or failed. This clarity allows the game master to design challenges and opportunities that align with your goals. Additionally, goals should have consequences for failure, whether it’s personal loss, a setback in the campaign, or changes in the world around you. This adds weight to your character’s choices and actions, making the game more immersive.

  • Achievability: A goal like "I want to become the most powerful wizard" is vague and hard to measure. A clearer goal would be, "I want to defeat an archmage in a duel to prove my mastery of magic."
  • Consequences: A failed goal could mean the loss of allies, the rise of a rival faction, or even damage to your character’s reputation or resources. This adds stakes to your decisions.

Collaborating with Your Party

As you set goals, think about how they might intersect with other characters in the party. Shared objectives or parallel paths can strengthen the group’s cohesion and create opportunities for collaborative storytelling. Goals don’t need to be identical, but they should complement each other, helping the party work toward common aims while allowing for individual character arcs. Discuss your goals with the group and explore how they can tie into the campaign’s larger narrative.

Implications

Your character’s goals will influence not only their personal journey but also how they interact with the world and other characters. Setting strong, clear goals helps drive the campaign forward, guiding your character’s actions and decisions. Here are some potential implications to consider:

  • Story Development: Your goals can provide natural story hooks for the DM, allowing them to weave your personal arc into the broader campaign. Whether you’re chasing lost artifacts, seeking revenge, or working to redeem a fallen house, these goals can become central plot points.
  • Party Dynamics: Your goals might align or clash with those of other party members. This can lead to cooperation, conflict, or even intrigue within the group, depending on how closely your objectives align with the party’s collective mission.
  • Motivation for Adventure: Clear goals give your character a reason to stay engaged in the campaign. They provide personal stakes, ensuring that your character has a vested interest in the outcome of missions and adventures. These motivations might shift over time as goals are achieved or new opportunities arise.
  • Character Growth: Your character’s goals will evolve as they grow through the campaign. Long-term goals might shift, or new priorities might emerge based on what happens in the story. Achieving or failing at a goal can lead to personal development, changing your character’s outlook or shaping their future ambitions.

Determining Connections with Other Characters

As you create your backstories, think about whether your characters have existing connections with one another. These connections aren’t required, but you're free to explore whether your characters have crossed paths before given the context of the starting of the campaign.

Some examples might be,

  • Shared experiences during the Last War: If any characters served together or found themselves on the same side, consider what they went through and how it shaped their relationship. Did they bond over a difficult situation, or was their time together marked by tension?
  • Unresolved debts or favors: Maybe one character owes another a debt or favor from a past encounter. This could be financial, personal, or something more abstract. How does this unresolved matter affect their current relationship?
  • Family ties or common origins: Perhaps your characters come from the same town, share a family connection, or belong to a similar cultural background. How did they know each other before the campaign, and what’s brought them together again?
  • Professional or personal history: Characters might have crossed paths professionally or personally in the past. Maybe they worked together, shared an adventure, or had a brief encounter. Was their previous experience positive, or did it end on uncertain terms?
